# The Epistle of the Name: Unified Writing Instructions

## Title & Structure

**Full Title:** The Epistle of the Name: A Letter to the Sprouting Church **Format:** One continuous letter divided into chapters and verses for readability (like biblical epistles) **Total Chapters:** 8 chapters **Purpose:** Written to be read aloud to congregations; rhythm, cadence, and rhetorical authority matter as much as content

---

## Part 1: Core Writing Principles

### 1. Sound Like Scripture, Not Like Lectures

**DO:**

- Write as Paul and John write: declarative, explosive, dense with theology
    
- Use short, punchy sentences that build momentum
    
- Build with Hebrew parallelism and repetition
    
- Break into spontaneous doxology when theology becomes overwhelming
    
- Use rhetorical questions that you immediately answer
    
- Ground every major claim in Scripture (through allusion and direct quotation)
    
- Write for the ear; this will be read aloud
    
- Echo biblical vocabulary and cadence naturally
    
- Let Scripture breathe through your words without constant citation
    

**DON'T:**

- Explain like a professor ("let me teach you about...")
    
- Use "I will show you..." preview statements
    
- Include table-of-contents style chapter previews
    
- Sound self-aware about being profound
    
- Use modern philosophical/academic language
    
- Use modern metaphors (electricity, radios, batteries, computers, etc.)
    
- Front-load complete definitions; let them spiral and deepen
    
- Put Hebrew words in the main text (footnotes only)
    
- Break the English flow with foreign terms
    

---

### 2. Pauline & Johannine Style Elements

#### Opening Moves (Like Paul):

- Explosive first sentence packed with dense theology
    
- Identify yourself by what God calls you, not by title
    
- Greeting includes theological payload
    
- **Example:** "Henry, a seed planted in Nigerian soil, to the Church scattered across every nation: grace to you and peace from God our Father..."
    

#### Logical Structure (Like Paul):

- Heavy use of "Therefore," "For," "Yet," "What then shall we say? and similar"
    
- Build ironclad logical chains connecting A to B to C
    
- Ask rhetorical questions: "What then? Shall we...? God forbid!"
    
- Multiple "If...then" constructions
    

#### Mystical Declaration (Like John):

- State paradoxes without immediately resolving them
    
- No apology for mystery
    
- Circular logic that somehow makes sense
    
- **Example:** "The Word was with God, and the Word was God"
    
- Let profound statements sit without over-explanation
    

#### Johannine Spiral (The Deepening Wave):

- Don't dump complete definitions in Chapter 1
    
- Introduce a concept (e.g., "The Name"), leave it, return to it later with more depth
    
- **Effect:** Feels like a deepening wave, not a lecture
    
- **Example:** Mention "The Name" in Ch 1, expand it in Ch 3, perfect it in Ch 8
    

#### Spontaneous Worship (Like Both):

- Break into doxology mid-argument when theology overwhelms
    
- Use deep Hebraic exclamations.
    
- Multiple "Amen" and "Selah" statements throughout (not just at end)
    
- Extended benedictions
    
- **New Addition:** Use "Amen" or "Selah" to end heavy revelations mid-chapter
    

- **Example:**
    
    v.12: "...for we are the very righteousness of God in Him."
    
    v.13: "This is true. Amen."
    

#### Apostolic Vocabulary:

- "Behold..." (Look at this!)
    
- "Know ye not...?" (Don't you know?)
    
- "What? know ye not...?" (What! Don't you understand?)
    
- "I would not have you ignorant..." (I don't want you to miss this)
    
- "Brethren, I speak..." (Listen carefully)
    
- "Hear this..."
    
- "Consider..."
    
- "It is written..."
    
- "Scripture says..."
    
- "Have ye not read...?"

### 3. Advanced Rhetorical Devices (The "Secret Sauce")

These tools create the subconscious authority that makes text "feel biblical."

#### A. The Principle of the "Interlocutor" (The Imaginary Debate)

Paul argues with an imaginary opponent (Romans 9:19: "You will say to me then..."). This creates dynamic tension.

**Instruction:** Anticipate the Nigerian religious objection and write it into the text.

**Example:** "You will say to me, 'are we not dust?' But I answer you, 'Is not the diamond also dust until the fire touches it?'"

**Application:** Address specific fears of the Nigerian church directly: their performance anxiety, their separation theology, their fear of becoming "too mystical."

#### B. The Hebraic "Chiasmus" (The Mirror Structure)

Hebrew structures sentences as A-B-B-A. This feels subconsciously "holy" to Bible readers.

**Instruction:** Occasionally invert your sentence structure for emphasis.

**Examples:**

- Linear: "The Seed reveals the Tree."
    
- Chiastic: "The Seed is hidden in the Soil; in the Soil, the Tree is revealed." (Seed → Soil → Soil → Tree)
    

**Effect:** Creates memorable, quotable phrases with poetic weight.

#### C. The "Rule of Three" (Triads)

Biblical writers love lists of three; it creates a sense of completeness.

**Instruction:** When listing attributes or actions, use three.

**Examples:**

- "He is the Root, the Branch, and the Fruit."
    
- "Not by might, nor by power, but by my Spirit."
    
- "Sons of the Soil, Children of the Day, Heirs of the Name."
    

#### D. "Biblical Resonance" (Allusion vs. Citation)

Bible-literate readers respond to echoes. Don't just quote the verse; use the vocabulary of the verse without always citing it.

**Instruction:** Use "Bible-soaked" language that echoes Scripture.

**Instead of:** "God will protect you in hard times." **Say:** "When you pass through the waters, He is there; and through the rivers, they shall not overflow you." (Echoing Isaiah 43 without stopping to cite it)

**Effect:** The text feels "thick" with Scripture even when not directly quoting. It bypasses the skeptical mind and lands in the spirit.

#### E. Vocabulary of Affection (Terms of Endearment)

Paul used "Brethren," "Beloved," "Little Children." Henry needs his own signature terms that fit the Seed/Soil metaphor.

**Your Signature Phrases:**

- "My fellow seeds"
    
- "Sons of the Soil"
    
- "Children of the Day"
    
- "Heirs of the Name"
    
- "Beloved boundaries"
    
- "Planted ones"
    
- "Branches of the One Tree"
    
- "You who sleep in the furrow"
    

**Why:** This creates a familial bond unique to this letter and reinforces the Seed Protocol framework organically.

### 4. Use Biblical Technical Vocabulary or Similar, Instead of Abstract Terms

When conveying deep theological/ontological concepts, **prefer biblical technical vocabulary over abstract philosophical language, to avoid sounding hellenistic**.

#### The Principle:

Rather than using generic abstract terms (even good ones like "substance," "being," "reality"), **use the specific biblical vocabulary that already exists for those concepts, or that Ancient Near Eastern Hebrews could use**.

#### Why This Works:

1. **Biblical precedent**: Paul and John used existing biblical vocabulary to carry ontological weight
    
2. **Relational clarity**: Biblical terms are inherently relational, not merely conceptual
    
3. **Scriptural grounding**: Readers immediately connect to the biblical text
    
4. **Avoids abstraction**: Keeps theology concrete and personal
    
5. **Sets up better footnotes**: You can then unpack the Hebrew/Greek depth
    

**The Method:**

1. Identify the theological concept you need to express (from our Glossary)
    
2. Search your biblical vocabulary for the technical term that carries that concept
    
3. Use that term (or similar) in the main text
    
4. Unpack the Hebrew/Greek/ontological depth in the footnote
    

**Remember:** Paul and John weren't inventing new vocabulary; they were loading existing biblical vocabulary with deeper meaning. Follow their pattern.

### 6. TSP Framework Integration

**KEY PRINCIPLE:** Use accessible English in the main text, Hebrew/TSP framework in footnotes.

#### In Main Text:

- Uses only English
    
- Biblical vocabulary and cadence
    
- Poetic and mysterious
    
- Lets the truth emerge naturally
    

#### In Footnotes (The "Mini-Sermons"):

- Introduce Hebrew terms with Paleo-Hebrew analysis
    
- Explain TSP framework concepts
    
- Connect to glossary entries
    
- Show how TSP reframes traditional understanding
    
- **New Addition:** Don't just give dictionary definitions; give implications
    
- Make footnotes a "treasure hunt for the hungry"
    

#### Examples:

**Instead of writing in text:**

> "You are the Church in [[NASHA|NASHA]], planted in mortal soil."

**Write:**

> "You are the Church under the veil¹, planted in mortal soil."

**Footnote:**

> ¹ **The Veil** - Hebrew: NASHA (נָשָׁא). Pictographically: Seed (נ) + Consume (שׁ) + First (א). Not deception or fall, but the intentional covering of Edenic awareness; the mind shifted from unity to multiplicity, enabling the Seed Protocol. The veil is not obstacle but method. As the dreamer forgets the waking world, so [[ADAM|Adam]] forgot the Glory. The awakening is not learning; it is remembering. See Genesis 3:13; 2 Corinthians 3:14-16.

  

**Instead of writing in text:**

> "This is [[ECHAD|Echad]]: the composite unity of God."

**Write:**

> "We are the One¹ expressing as many."

**Footnote:**

> ¹ **The One** - Hebrew: ECHAD (אֶחָד). Not solitary unity (YACHID) but composite unity: the unified whole expressed through distinct parts. Like "one flesh" (Genesis 2:24) or "one body, many members" (1 Corinthians 12:12). God is not alone; God is unified-while-multiple. This is the mystery of "the Lord our God, the Lord is One" (Deuteronomy 6:4). You are not becoming one with God; you are discovering you never left.

### 7. Coin Punchier Scripture-Like Lines

Instead of quoting Scripture verbatim, **distill the essence** into your own prophetic voice.

#### Example:

**Scripture says:**

> "For in him we live, and move, and have our being" (Acts 17:28)

**Don't write:**

> "Acts 17:28 says we live and move and have our being in God."

**Instead write:**

> "You do not live near God, as a neighbor in the next house. You live in God, as the fish lives in the water. Remove the water, and the fish is not merely separated—it vanishes."

**Effect:**

- The biblical truth is there
    
- But in YOUR voice
    
- More memorable
    
- More powerful
    
- Those who know Scripture will hear the echo

### 9. Scripture Citation System: Alpha-Numeric Coding

#### Letters (a, b, c) = Cross-References

Point to other Scripture passages that relate to the concept being discussed.

**Format:**

> "For God raised us up together and made us sit together in heavenly placesᵃ in Christ."

**Footer:**

> ᵃ Ephesians 2:6; Colossians 3:1-3; Romans 6:4-5

  

#### Numbers (1, 2, 3) = TSP Framework Notes

Explain:

- Glossary terms from TSP Theological Glossary
    
- Hebrew insights (Paleo-Hebrew pictographic meanings)
    
- Framework clarification (how TSP reframes the concept)
    
- Implications, not just definitions
    

**Format:**

> "This is not about closing distance. This is about the veil² being lifted."

**Footer:**

> ² **The Veil** - Not ignorance but NASHA's designed covering, the boundary-consciousness that allows God to experience limitation. What appears as separation is actually divine self-bracketing: Echad expressing through multiplicity. The veil isn't obstacle but method. When Moses turned aside to see the burning bush, the veil thinned. When you see Christ, the veil is gone. See 2 Corinthians 3:14-16; Hebrews 6:19; Matthew 27:51.

  

#### How to Reference Scripture in Main Text:

**When directly quoting:**

- "It is written..."
    
- "Scripture says..."
    
- "Moses said..."
    
- "David himself declared..."
    
- "The prophet spoke..."
    
- "Paul writes..."
    

**When alluding:**

- Just state it with footnote reference
    
- No need to always name the book/chapter inline
    
- Use biblical resonance; speak in Scripture-like vocabulary
    
- **Example:** "Were you not crucified with Christᵃ?" (footnote: ᵃ Romans 6:6; Galatians 2:20)

### 10. Chapter Transitions

**DO NOT:**

- Preview upcoming chapters like a textbook ("In Chapter 2, I will discuss...")
    
- Use section headers like "What I Will Show You"
    
- Announce your outline
    
- Sound like a professor introducing syllabus topics
    

**DO:**

- End chapters naturally where the thought completes
    
- Use simple transitional verses that pose the question next chapter answers
    
- Let chapters flow into each other organically
    
- Trust the reader to follow without roadmap announcements
    

**Example of Good Transition (End of Chapter 1):**

> "What then of the Garden? What of Adam? What of the seed that Scripture says was planted in the ground? Hear now what was hidden from the beginning."

Then Chapter 2 begins answering those questions directly.

#### Chapter Endings:

- Natural transition (don't announce next chapter)
    
- Sometimes end with a question
    
- Sometimes end with a doxology
    
- Sometimes end mid-thought (the spiral continues)
    

**Examples:**

**Ending with question:**

> "But you ask, 'If we are this glorious seed, why do we suffer? Why do we die? Why was the seed buried in the dark earth of [[EDEN|Eden]]?'"

**Ending with doxology:**

> "To the King eternal, immortal, invisible, the only wise God, be honor and glory for ever and ever. Amen."

**Ending mid-thought (spiral continues):**

> "But how does this glory walk on two legs? How does the Mystery wear the clothes of the mother, the pastor, and the child? Let us now look at the order of the House."

### 11. Tone & Voice

#### Punctuation Rules:

**No em dashes (—)** - Biblical epistles don't use them. Use commas, semicolons, or periods instead. Break long thoughts into separate sentences rather than extending with dashes.

**Examples:**

- ❌ "The flesh wars against the Spirit—and the Spirit against the flesh—so that you cannot do what you wish."
    
- ✓ "The flesh wars against the Spirit, and the Spirit against the flesh: so that you cannot do what you wish."
    
- ✓ "The flesh wars against the Spirit; the Spirit wars against the flesh. You cannot do what you wish."
    

#### Your Distinctive Voice:

- Nigerian prophetic urgency without apology
    
- Pastoral warmth combined with theological precision
    
- Intellectual rigor serving love, not displaying expertise
    
- Paul's passion without Paul's harshness
    
- Writing for generational transmission, not just immediate audience
    
- Using the vocabulary of affection specific to the Seed/Soil metaphor
    
- Hebrew heart expressed in English tongue
    

#### Purpose:

- Love-driven identity revelation, not condemnation
    
- Awakening the Church to who she already is
    
- Using linguistic analysis as shovel, not treasure
    
- Making complex theology accessible without dumbing it down
    

#### Remember:

- You're writing as fellow boundary, not external authority
    
- Use "we" and "us" as much as "you"
    
- Personal vulnerability when appropriate
    
- Direct address to specific groups (pastors, youth, suffering, scholars)
    
- Address the specific fears and objections of the Nigerian church through the interlocutor device

### 12. Verse Numbering

**Purpose:** For memorization and quotation (like actual Scripture)

**Format:** Each verse should be:

- A complete thought unit
    
- Quotable on its own
    
- Flows naturally into the next verse
    
- Generally 1-3 sentences per verse
    
- Occasionally just a single affirmation after heavy revelation: "This is true. Amen."
    

**Numbering Style:**

- Continuous through each chapter
    
- Chapter 1: verses 1-36 (or however many needed)
    
- Chapter 2: verses 1-40 (etc.)

## Part 2: Content Guardrails

### What to ALWAYS Include:

1. **Biblical allusions and echoes** - Let Scripture breathe through your words
    
2. **Hebrew parallelism** - Repeat ideas in slightly different ways
    
3. **Paraconsistent logic** - Hold seemingly contradictory truths simultaneously
    
4. **Doxological breaks** - Worship when theology overwhelms
    
5. **Personal warmth** - This is a letter to real people you love
    
6. **Identity over behavior** - Revelation precedes transformation
    
7. **Monergistic theology** - God's action, not human effort
    
8. **Echad perspective** - Unity expressing through multiplicity
    
9. **Triads** - Lists of three for rhythmic completeness
    
10. **Chiasmus** - Mirror structures for emphasis
    
11. **Biblical resonance** - Echo Scripture's vocabulary without always citing
    
12. **Vocabulary of affection** - "Fellow fruits," "Sons of the Vine," etc.
    
13. **The interlocutor** - Anticipate and answer objections within the text
    
14. **Ancient/biblical imagery only** - No modern metaphors
    

### What to NEVER Include:

1. **Dualistic Western frameworks** - No separation theology
    
2. **Performance-based religion** - No earning, achieving, becoming
    
3. **Academic showing off** - Scholarship serves love, not ego
    
4. **New Age terminology** - Use biblical vocabulary only
    
5. **Modern technological metaphors** - No batteries, radios, computers, etc.
    
6. **Hebrew words in main text** - Footnotes only
    
7. **Guilt manipulation** - Love-driven revelation, not shame
    
8. **Preview announcements** - No "In the next chapter I will..."
    
9. **Over-explanation** - Trust the reader; state and move on
    
10. **Apology for mystery** - Paradox is feature, not bug
    
11. **Complete definitions upfront** - Let concepts spiral and deepen
    
12. **Clinical language** - Use biblical emotional vocabulary

## Part 3: Practical Writing Process

### For Each Chapter:

1. **Start with explosive opening** - Dense theology in first few verses
    
2. **Build with connectors "e.g. Therefore/For/Yet e.t.c" logic** - Show the connections
    
3. **Use rhetorical questions** - "What then shall we say?" e.t.c
    
4. **Quote/allude to Scripture constantly** - Show biblical grounding
    
5. **Use biblical resonance** - Echo Scripture's vocabulary without constant citation
    
6. **Employ the interlocutor** - Argue with imagined objections
    
7. **Use triads and chiasmus** - Create rhythmic memorability
    
8. **Address them with affection** - "Fellow seeds," "Heirs of the Name"
    
9. **Break into doxology** - When theology becomes overwhelming
    
10. **Use "Amen" or "Selah"** - To punctuate heavy revelations
    
11. **Use apostolic-like vocabulary** - "Behold," "Know ye not," etc.
    
12. **End with simple transition** - Pose question next chapter answers
    

### For Footnotes:

1. **Cross-references (letters):** List relevant Scripture passages
    
2. **Framework notes (numbers):**
    

- Give Hebrew term with Paleo-Hebrew breakdown
    
- Explain TSP Glossary reframe in 2-3 sentences
    
- Give implications, not just definitions - Make it a mini-sermon
    
- Cross-reference to glossary if applicable
    
- Include relevant Scripture showing biblical basis
    
- Make it a treasure hunt for the hungry reader
